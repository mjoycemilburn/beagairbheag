'Her father'

Remember, that, when the third person possessive 'a' is followed by a word beginning with a vowel, the masculine possessive is elided (omitted) and the feminine one is followed by 'h-'. So 'his father' = 'athair' and 'her father ' 'a h-athair'.

1. 'air falbh' 'away' may have a different interpretation, as you shall see later on in the passage.

2. 'at the lamb sale' A simple enough phrase, seemingly, but full of pitfalls for the unwary.

Now, obviously more than one lamb is being sold, so we are dealing with a sale of lambs rather than a sale of a lamb. So we have to think of lambs in the plural.

The word used for a 'sale' of livestock is 'fèill' which originally meant a 'holy day' e.g. Fèill Anndrais etc., then 'fair' etc. So 'a lamb sale' really means 'a lambs' sale' or ' a sale of lambs'. The latter form is the one used by Gaelic i.e. 'fèill uan' (or fèill-uan).

"But wait", I hear you cry, "isn't 'uan' singular? Well, yes, but in nouns of this type i.e. masculine nouns of the first declension (class / type of noun) the form for the genitive plural is the same as that for the radical case (nominative singular) So 'uan' = 'a lamb' or 'of lambs' according to context, the nom. and dat. plural being 'uain', the same as the gen. singular. This isn't as confusing as it looks, as context will indicate the correct meaning.

So 'a lamb sale' is 'féill uan'. Fair enough, but what we want to say is 'THE lamb sale'. Now, you will note that the 'the' in English is attached to the word 'lamb', and not to the word 'sale'. The same thing happens in Gaelic, but the order is reversed, and this becomes [the] sale of the lambs. I have bracketed the first 'the' because this does not occur in the Gaelic version. Therefore 'féill nan uan'.

Dàibhidh's (and this is what you were aiming for, Frank) 'aig an fhèill-uan' seems perfectly possible, given the form 'fèill-uan', but I've never actually seen it. Anybody???

So what's wrong with 'féill-uain'? Well, as I have already pointed out, 'uain' would imply that only one lamb was being sold.

"But I'm sure I've seen 'féill-chruidh' for 'cattle sale' " someone objects. Yes, you may well have, but 'cruidh' is the gen. sing. of 'crodh' ('cattle') which is a collective noun, and collective nouns in Gaelic are treated a little differently from other nouns. Though they have a singular form, they have a plural meaning, and are often treated as if they were plural. (See 'clann' and 'crodh' in my dictionary)

Sometimes 'fèill reic' is used as in

'cha bhi fèill reic nan laogh ann am Port Rìgh mar as àbhaist anns an fhoghar' - 'the calf sale will not be in Portree as usual in the autumn' (Scotsman) The word 'reic' cannot be used itself for this kind of 'sale'. Cf the following:

a' crochadh air reic nan uan - depending (for income) on the sale of lambs (i.e. the selling of lambs, not the lamb sale)

Most adverbs in English are formed by suffixing the corresponding adjective with 'ly'. Thus the adjective 'sudden' becomes 'suddenly'. Similarly, most adverbs in Gaelic are formed by placing the particle 'gu' before the corresponding adjective, so 'mall' 'slow' becomes 'gu mall' 'slowly'. When the adjective begins with a vowel an 'h-' is inserted, so 'obann' 'sudden' becomes 'gu h-obann' 'suddenly'. 'Gu grad' may also mean 'suddenly', though it often also means 'quickly'. The expression ''sa bhad', however, means 'on the spot', 'at once', 'immediately'. Though not common, the term 'a chlisge' is not unknown.

'she rose' (or 'she got up') = dh'èirich i'. Note that the particle 'dh' is inserted before verbs beginning with a vowel (or a vowel sound) in the past tense. Verbs beginning with a consonant simply lenite where possible. So

'dh'èigh e' - 'he shouted',

dh'fhàg i e' - 'she left him'

'chaill e e' - 'he lost it' , and

'lean e mi' - 'he followed me'.

The past tense of the irregular verb 'rach', 'go' is 'chaidh'. 'Up', when preceded by a verb of motion is 'suas' (in this particular situation, but see below). Brian went 'down', while Rùra, Raibeart and Magaidh went neither up nor down!

Karen, however, has opened a whole new can of worms (such a charming expression this, isn't it?) by using 'a-nìos'.This is a complicated subject, which is why I gave such a lot of space to it in my dictionary. Words such as 'suas', sìos, 'a-nuas', 'a-nìos etc. (I won't deal with them all, as it would take too long) describe events from the viewpoint of the subject of the narrative. The subject here was initially standing at the foot of the stairs and, from her viewpoint she went 'up' (suas). If someone had been at the top of the stairs, looking down, she would have thought of the person ascending as not coming 'up' but coming 'from down' (a-nìos) If we reverse the action, so that the person upstairs is (from her own point of view) going down, then she would use 'sìos'. The person at the foot of the stairs would see the descending person as coming 'from up' (a-nuas). To cut a long story short, Karen, you can't use 'a-nìos' here. Nor can you have 'chaidh' and ' a-nìos' used together!

'To', when it means 'to' and 'into' is 'do'. After the possessive 'a' meaning 'her' the noun is not lenited, so 'do a seòmar' '(in)to her room'. Following this with 'fhéin' makes it 'her own room' (always 'fhèin', Frank, whether referring to a man or a woman).

Alternatively, you may say 'dhan t-seòmar aice-fhèin' / don t-seòmar aice-fhèin ('dhan' is a common variant of 'don' which means 'to the' or 'into the').

Because 'seòmar' begins with 's' + a vowel, it becomes 't-seòmar' in the definite dative case (after 'don).

The preposition 'chun', Raibeart, may only be used with a definite noun in the genitive case, but, as it means 'as far as, but not into', it cannot be used here.

Likewise, Karen, 'gus', as a preposition, is used only with definite nouns, and wouldn't be used for going 'into' a place.

You will note (no pun intended!) that people are split into two camps, those who think 'pounds' should be singular in Gaelic, and those who think it should be plural. Now, a few commonly counted objects are usually in the singular, e.g. bliadhna, duine, latha, mìle ('thousands' or 'miles') and sgillinn. The usual word nowadays for 'pound' (money) is 'not', with the plural 'notaichean'. The word 'punnd' (without an accent though it has a long vowel) was formerly used along with the word 'Sassanach' (to distinguish it from the old Scots pound), with the plural 'puinnd Shassanach'.

The word for 'shilling' is 'tastan', with the plural 'tastain' or 'tastanan' . Those who used 'sgillinn' for 'shilling' made a very understandable error, as this word originally did mean 'shilling', with the word 'peighinn' being used for 'penny'. Upon the union of the two countries a Scots shilling was worth one twelfth of an English shilling i.e. one English penny. So what had been called a 'shilling' was now worth a penny.

The word 'tastan' (older spelling 'tasdan' was then used for 'shilling'. 'A few shillings' is 'beagan thastan', and, if you use 'tastanan' as the plural, this could be 'beagan thastanan'.

As I already mentioned, the word for pound is 'not', with the plural 'notaichean'. This follows the normal rules for counting, so we should have 'deich notaichean' (or 'deich puinnd Shassanach').
