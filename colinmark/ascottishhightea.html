A Scottish High Tea



Passage for Translation 4 : 12/3/06

Below is my translation of a passage taken from a piece written by a native speaker. Your task, if you accept it, is to translate it into Gaelic. Though it’s far from ‘Mission Impossible’, there are some interesting and useful idioms, as well as useful vocabulary, involved.

Anyone who feels that this passage is within his/her grasp may have a go.

The participants may translate the whole passage in one go, or do it section by section which I will set week by week. Each week I will comment on the participants’ translations for the previous week.

The translations should be sent directly to me. I will collate them and comment where necessary on them, one preset section at a time.

Anybody else is, of course, free to join in the discussion, with questions, suggestions or other comments.

For those who want to translate the whole passage at once, I must warn you that there are occasions when a point brought up early in the discussion of the passage may have a bearing on a later section of the passage. If you have made an error you won’t be able to change the same error in a later section.

Here is the passage, and, if nothing else, you will learn how to set out a Scottish high tea for a visitor!

Now, before my wife had* left, she had* arranged the small dining-table in the back porch so that I could take my meals on it until she returned. This left our dining-room neat and clean for anyone who came to look in on me. In addition to this, she had* put a nice mat on the dining table in it and a dish full of flowers in the middle of it so as to be smart (looking). Anyway, I began to prepare the tea for the English woman like this: I took away the dish in which the flowers were, doubled the mat to one end of the table and opened the press where the wife kept the (table)-cloths. I reached for the first one I saw, and when I unfolded it, it was particularly fine, with pictures of wild, many-coloured birds and beasts, and written on it, in English “Sanctuary of Corrumbura, Oueensland." A (table-)cloth which a relative had* sent us from Australia. I spread it on the table.

Then I opened the glass cabinet (i.e.glass cupboard/press) in which the wife kept china dishes, and took out a cup, a saucer, a small plate and a bread plate. I set them on the table along with a knife and a tea-spoon. After this I set bread, cakes and biscuits on it, along with dishes with butter, sugar, jam and milk. Then I found a new, elegant, four-cup tea-pot, and went with it to the back-porch, where the kettle was furiously whistling as it boiled. I scalded the tea-pot, put tea in it and filled it with boiling water. I put a tea-cosy on it, and set it on the table in the dining-room, with a saucer under it.

*the author uses the simple past tense for this i.e. ‘Now, before my wife left, she arranged etc.’ which may seem a bit odd in English, but is perfectly normal in Gaelic, is simpler, and, indeed, more elegant. All words within brackets are mine (by way of explanation) and should not be translated.

The first section is:

Now, before my wife had* left, she had* arranged the small dining-table in the back porch so that I could take my meals on it until she returned.

This should reach me by Friday 17th.

Everybody got the introductory nis(e) / a nis(e) correct. Rù-ra's 'ma-tha' is a slight elaboration, but is perfectly natural here.

Now, either 'mus' or mun' may be used for 'before', but the important point is that both need 'do' before the past tense i.e. either 'mus do' or 'mun do'.

Both 'dh' fhàg' and 'dh' fhalbh' are correct for 'left' / 'had left'. There is often some uncertainty about which verb to use in a given situation. The important point is that, if a place or an item is mentioned, 'fàg' must be used, but if not, then either may be used.

Everybody had 'mo bean' and this is, of course, perfectly correct. The alternative, 'a' bhean agam' is far less common, but is used occasionally e.g. as in Derick Thomson's farewell editorial in Gairm 2000:

Facal taing an toiseach do luchd­ teaghlaich, a bha a' cuideachadh ann an iomadach dòigh, agus gu h-àraid a' bhean agam, Carol, a bha a' toirt taic dhomh fad nan leth-cheud bliadhna

And now for: 'she had arranged the small dining-table'. Everyone except Karen and Brian chose to use the idiom 'cuir air dòigh' (as the author did), though in slightly differing ways.

Karen chose to use - or intended to use :-) -'shuidhich' which means 'set' / 'set up', 'placed' etc. and is fine here (IMO). Brian wrote 'chur i di\reach' which, as well as having the slight spelling error in 'chuir' would mean 'set upright' or 'set straight'. Now, we'll get back to the positioning of 'air dòigh' in a moment. First we'll deal with 'the small dining-table'. If you had 'am bòrd-bìdh beag', then you were home and dry. Brian was almost there with 'am bòrd-bidhe' ('bidhe' often appears without an accent), but forgot the 'beag'. So what is wrong with the others? Frank's 'am bòrd beag bidh' means 'the little food table', while Ealasaid's 'bòrd a' bhidhe beag' means (almost!) 'the table of the small food'. Karen's 'an bórd beag a'bhidhe' is wrong for a number of reasons. First of all 'the table' would be 'am bòrd'. But in the arrangement you have used, you wouldn't need the 'am' at all. This would simply be 'bórd beag a' bhidhe'. But his would mean 'the little table of the food. We really need the compound noun 'bòrd-bidh(e) with the adjective following the noun as it would normally do i.e. 'am bòrd-bidhe beag'.

Everyone except Frank had 'in the porch' correct. I have to object to Frank's on the grounds of spelling, as 'phords' would have to be pronounced 'fords'.

With regard to the word 'back' in 'back porch', this raises a couple of questions. Everyone except Brian, who apparently has a habit of ignoring adjectives :-), used the correct word 'cùl', but, unfortunately, as it is being used descriptively (and is really the genitive singular of 'cùl'), it has to be slenderised to 'cùil'. Our main difficulty here is that some speakers regard nouns used in this way as an adjective and lenite them or leave them unlenited accordingly, while others never lenite them i.e. they remain as nouns. Thus you may have òrain ghaoil' or 'òrain gaoil'. "Aha!" some may say, "isn't that simply the difference between 'love songs' and 'songs of love'? Well, no, as this happens with many other such nouns. I cannot say 'all' since I simply don't know! If we take the descriptive noun in question i.e. 'cùil' we can have:

ball air na beingean-cùil back-bench member

being-chùil back bench n (both from Faclair na Pàrlamaid)

[...] agus feumaidh e tighinn a-staigh air an doras cùil air eagal 's gum faic Ceit (Dolina Nic'illInnein) e. [...] and he has to enter by the back door in case Kate (Dolina MacLennan) sees him. Criosaidh Dick - The Scotsman (review of TV programme)

Ma tha mi ag iarraidh prògram sònraichte a chluinntinn feumaidh mi éisdeachd ris anns a' chàr aig an doras chùil. Tha deagh reception an-sin. If I want to hear a particular programme I must listen to it in the car at the back door. There's good reception there. Criosaidh Dick - The Scotsman (about radio reception in the Highlands)

Sharp-eyed readers will observe that in one example 'cùil' is lenited, and in the other, by the same writer, it is not. This may be the result of editorial imput - who knows? Both are in the definite dative case, and an ordinary adjective would be lenited if possible.

Personally, I prefer treating a descriptive noun like this as an adjective and would lenite it where necessary.

The other difficulty is whether there needs to be a hyphen or not. There's no problem with words such as 'being-chùil 'back bench' above, as 'back bench' is a new entity, and not simply a 'bench at the back'. On the other hand, 'a back door' is simply a'a door which happens to be at the back', not a new thing called a 'back-door'. Thus, I would write 'doras cùil'. In addition, you cannot write it all as one word as both elements have equal stress.

'I could take'. I tend to think of 'b' urrainn dhomh / dha' etc. as meaning 'would have the mental / physical ability' as opposed to there being a possibility or probability i.e. 'so that I might take'. One or two peple used the future tense, which doesn't work here.

As previously, both 'an sin' and 'an uairsin / an uair sin' are used for 'then' When 'fhuair' means 'found', it usually means 'found by chance, 'happened upon'. When something is found by looking for it, it's better to use 'lorg'. Magaidh's 'fhuair mi lorg air' is fine.

As would be expected, the phrase 'a new, elegant, four-cup tea-pot' caused some difficulty. I think it would be best, Rù-rà, to leave 'four-cup' until last, as the others and the author do. Magaidh, though, left it out altogether! :-) Both Nansaidh and Brian used 'de' here, which is not recommended, as 'de' in this kind of context implies 'consisting of'. The plural of 'cupa' / 'cupan' is usually 'cupan(n)an, though 'cupain' is possible.

The author has: Lorg mi an sin poit-teatha ùr ghrinn cheithir cupannan

All the words used for elegant would do reasonably well.

The phrase 'was furiously whistling' suggests continuous action, and so we can't use the past perfect 'ghoil e', but must use 'bha e a' goil'. We appear to have most variations of the Gaelic for 'whistling', but they are all correct! With regard to 'deanadh' for 'deanamh', I know of only one person who uses it. Any comments on this? I'm not so sure about the choices for 'furiously' though. I feel that they would be applied to people rather than things. The author uses 'gu dian'. For 'as it boiled', correct choices would be 'fhad 's a bha e a' goil', 'mar a bha e a' goil (spelling, Ealasaid!) or ' 's e a' goil'

The author has 'far an robh an coire gu dian a' feadaireachd agus e a' goil.'
