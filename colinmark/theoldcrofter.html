Comments:

We can deal with the first sentence as a single unit. There are two ways of dealing with this:

Tha e na rathad fada, dìreach, cumhang. (literally: It is in its long, straight, narrow road)

or

'S e rathad fada, dìreach, cumhang a th' ann. (literally: It's a long, straight, narrow road that is in it.)

Both are commonly used when describing people or things, but the second, using the assertive verb, is more emphatic, and more likely to be used here. Traditionally, there would be no commas between the adjectives in Gaelic, but the use of commas is becoming more and more common. They are used in the original, so I have retained them.

When saying that one person or thing is another e.g. a noun is a noun, or a pronoun is a noun etc you cannot use 'tha' directly. E.g.:

1 . James is a teacher.

You CANNOT say 'Tha Seumas tidsear'.

You must either say:

'Tha Seumas na thidsear' (lit. James is in his teacher)

or

'S e tidsear a th' ann an Seumas. (lit. it's a teacher that is in James)

2. Margaret is a doctor.

'S e dotair a th' ann am Mairead.

3. He is a teacher and she is a nurse.

'S e tidsear a th' ann agus 's e dotair a th' innte. (lit. It's a teacher that is in him and it's a doctor that is in her.)

So what about Brian's 'Se rathad fada, dìreach, cumhang a tha e'?

This may look alright, but it breaks the 'something is a something' rule I mentioned earlier. In fact, this 'a tha e' phrase is used in a construction which is almost the mirror image of the "S e tidsear a th' ann' type of construction. It begins with 's ann (or sann) and ends with 'a tha e'. It is used in descriptions where adjectives (without nouns) are used, or other phrases not including nouns. Here are two examples:

Leis an fhìrinn innse, sann gu math mall, tioram a tha e. - To tell the truth, it's quite slow and dry. (of a book) - Ailig O Hianlaidh in the Scotsman.

Sann don òigridh a tha e, agus chan, eil aois ann cho doirbh program a dhèanamh dhaibh. - It's for [the] youth, and there isn't a age so difficult to make a programme for [them]. - Criosaidh Dick in the Scotsman.

The square brackets indicate words which are in the Gaelic, but not the English version.

The author of the original could have mentioned the road in an earlier sentence, and then gone on to say:

Passage for Translation – The Old Crofter Section 3c : 22/8/08


Rick's 'Chuimhnich feadhainn an Croitear gu math' presents us with a problem which doesn't arise in English (or does it?)

Compare these two sentences.

1. I remember where I left it now.

2. I remember him very well.

In 1. we have a sudden, on the spot, one-off memory – something which has just occurred to the person.

In 2. we clearly have a different situation. The 'remembering' is a permanent, ongoing process. While not always at the forefront of the person's mind, the memory is always there.

'Chuimhnich' would imply a sudden memory. e.g. Gu h-obann, chuimhnich mi nach do ghlas mi an doras - suddenly I remembered that I hadn't locked the door.

In addition, 'cuimhnich' meaning 'remember' or 'recall' needs 'air' before a noun, or a prepositional pronoun of 'air' when a personal pronoun is involved, e.g.

nuair a chuimhnicheas sinn air - when we remember it / him ... Note that a Future Tense (in this case the Relative Future) is used here because the remembering will presumably be repetitive, though the English version has a Present Tense.

Remember that 'deagh' is a special sort of adjective. It is always attached to a noun, always precedes the noun and lenites it where possible.
